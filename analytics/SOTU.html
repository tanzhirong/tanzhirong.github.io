Analysis of USA's State of the Union Addresses¶

This exercise attempts to better understand speech patterns of USA's SOTU addresses across time thorough natural language processing and unsupervised learning techniques (such as TF-IDF, PCA, and K-means clustering).

Data Processing

with open('speeches.json', 'r') as f:
    speeches = json.loads(f.read())

#Separate the speeches into sentences and tokenize the words.
for i in range(len(speeches)):
    speeches[i]['sentence'] = sent_tokenize(speeches[i]['text'])
    for sentence_i in range(len(speeches[i]['sentence'])):
        speeches[i]['sentence'][sentence_i] = 
        speeches[i]['sentence'][sentence_i].rstrip("\n\r")
    #Create template of dimension (#sentences)
    speeches[i]['word'] = np.empty([len(speeches[i]['sentence'])])
    for j in range(len(speeches[i]['sentence'])):
        #Find number of words
        speeches[i]['word'][j]=len(word_tokenize(speeches[i]
                                                 ['sentence'][j]))

#Create list of attributes by year, to understand how the number of
#sentences and average length of speeches have changed over the years.
which_year_list = np.empty(226)
num_sentences_list = np.empty(226)
average_length_list = np.empty(226)
for yr in range(226):
    num_sentences_list[yr] = len(speeches[yr]['sentence'])
    average_length_list[yr] = stat.mean(speeches[yr]['word'])
    which_year_list[yr] = speeches[yr]['year']

def clean_and_split(s):
    """
    Input: uncleaned text
    Output: cleaned text covered to bag of words
    """
    #covert to lower case, translate to white space
    s = s.lower().replace('-',' ').translate(s.maketrans(" ",
    " ",string.punctuation))
    # replace \r\n
    s = re.sub('(\r\n)+',' ',s)
    # replace whitespace substrings with one whitespace and remove
    # leading/trailing whitespaces
    s = re.sub(' +',' ',s.strip())
    return s.split(' ')

cleaned_text = [clean_and_split(speeches[i]['text']) for i in range(226)]

#Obtain dictionary / list of unique words
dictionaryA = corpora.Dictionary(cleaned_text)

#Use package to find Tf-idf values
TFIDF = TfidfModel(corpus_list)

#We want to transform it into a Matrix where
#Row i = document i, column j = jth word in dictionary
def transform_TFIDF(document):
    row_d = np.zeros(len(dictionaryA))
    for i in range(len(dictionaryA)):
        if i in [word[0] for word in document]:
            row_d[i]=[word[1] for word in document if word[0] == i][0]
        else:
            row_d[i]=0
    return(row_d)

TFIDF_mat = np.zeros((226, len(dictionaryA)))
for i in range(len(speeches)):
    TFIDF_mat[i] = transform_TFIDF(TFIDF[corpus_list[i]])

def similarity(A_doc, B_doc):
    numerator = np.inner(TFIDF_mat[A_doc], TFIDF_mat[B_doc])
    denom = np.linalg.norm(TFIDF_mat[A_doc])*
            np.linalg.norm(TFIDF_mat[B_doc])
    return(numerator/denom)

We observe that similarity of speeches can be predicted/are related by either:

  1. Sequence in time (i.e. speeches closer in date are more similar). For example, Barack Obama's speeches are more similar to Bill Clinton or George Bush than to Lincoln. Likewise, a speech in the 1800s will be more similar to another speech in the 1800s than 2000s. This is due to linguistic differences. Over time, the English language / word choice will change, preferences for certain style will evolve, and this is reflected in the similarities as well. Likewise, the focus in politics shift over time. For example, speeches in 1800s may talk about independence and slavery, while that in 2000s talk about the war on terror.

  2. Party. For example, Obama's speeches are more similar to Bill Clinton's, rather than George Bush, a different party president. This can be explained by the party focus - i.e. Obama and Clinton may focus on different policy issues as democrats compared to the Republicans.

To understand how can we construct a better similarity measure, we first need to understand what are we trying to measure in terms of saying that 2 documents are similar - are they similar in subject matter, or in linguistic style? Our method seems to focus on the 2nd one.

Observe from the similarity by the same president, that the speeches most similar are all by very recent presidents - Bush, Obama, Clinton, Johnson. There are a few possibilities:

  1. The packages we employ (e.g. gensim) are biased towards (i.e. more able to recognize) today's words than those in the 1800s and early 1900s due to usage and word style. Hence, a better similarity measure will not be too dependent on the dictionary.

  2. Length of speech affects similarity. The length of speech increases with year. Hence, this implies that the dictionary may be more influenced by words from recent years than in the past. A way to improve this is to ensure the length of all speeches are the same - perhaps by selecting a subset from each speech such that the length is normalized.

  3. Whether the speech is written or spoken may affect similarity too. Speeches spoken may be more similar due to the spoken style, compared to written documents, which can take a larger variation. The only way to address this is to compare written or spoken speeches separately.

The above points also cover ways to construct a better similarity matrix. Other ways may include taking into consideration the sequences of words (i.e. word embeddings) instead of a bag-of-words model, since a same word may mean different things in 2 eras.
